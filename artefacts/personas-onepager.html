1 ▶
Crew member
Crew		 14–18, avg ~16. Casual, hourly, low wage. Transient — high turnover, rolling hires every 1–2 weeks. Low Temporary, low-wage, low investment in store's long-term success. Trained & "verified" on one or more of: Front Counter, McCafé, Drive-Thru, Kitchen, Customer Areas (cleaning). Follow station scripts, hit KDS timers, surface issues verbally to the shift manager.
Do NOT log incidents. Paul explicitly removed crew logging in Donesafe — produced "an unmanageable flood of submissions" historically.		 Same as licensee.

Why crew should NOT be the primary user of any platform

  • No SSO. Crew lack standardised single-sign-on; where it exists it's far less integrated than corporate management access.
  • No devices. No personal device access during shift; no shared crew-facing device assigned to them.
  • No pockets. Uniform deliberately has only fake stitched pockets — designed to prevent phone use during shift. Dual rationale: focus + risk of phones in oil vats.
  • Low care factor + casual = they're the object of compliance, not the agent. Don't make them log; make them notify the shift manager (which is already what happens).

Demographic colour

  • Weekday 9–5 in school terms skews 20s+; evenings, weekends, school holidays skew teenage.
  • Ben started at 13y 9m.
  • Ben's era: front-of-house skewed female + "talkative guys"; kitchen skewed male. [DATED — DEI posture has shifted since 2017]
  • Track preference (front / back / cafe) nominally asked at interview but in practice you go where the store needs you.

Paper carbon-copy SOC → filed in binder → rarely referenced is the legacy state. Migration to iPad-based digital SOCs is partially underway inside McD already. Worth confirming with Paul/Kathy how far it's progressed.

3 ▶
Shift manager
First management rung · usually a uni student
Primary demo persona
 19–25. Hourly or low-tier salary; most are part-time uni students. Always promoted from crew — never hired directly into the role. Max ~10 per store. Low to medium Primary motivation: "don't get in trouble." Maintain a smooth, trouble-free shift; minimise conflict; avoid scrutiny from the RM. Most aim for minimum-expectations-met, not driving performance. Run the shift end-to-end: station allocation, KDS/timer monitoring, escalations, opens/closes, cash & deposits, youth-labour compliance, first-responder on safety events.
Logs incidents/hazards in Donesafe today (browser pinned to phone/iPad). Generates corrective actions. Paul's onus on capture: crew notify → shift manager logs. [Gong]		 Same as licensee.

The "verbal-to-shift-manager-to-forgotten" pattern

This is named explicitly as the dominant failure mode for incident reporting:

  1. Incident happens (oil spill, equipment failure, near-miss)
  2. Crew verbally informs the shift manager mid-task
  3. Shift manager might jot a note — more commonly, forgotten amidst constant demands of the shift
  4. No formal log created. No follow-up. No systemic cause surfaced.

The bridge from "noticing" to "documented" is a single overloaded human carrier running a peak shift.

Pitch implication — the load-bearing demo point

Anything that collapses the report-creation friction directly attacks this failure mode:

  • Voice-to-text on a manager's already-in-hand device
  • Instant photo capture
  • Low/zero mandatory fields at capture time
  • AI populating the form post-hoc

The system has to make it faster to log than to forget.

Big behavioural delta between shift-mgr-led shifts (quiet periods — Sunday nights, overnight) and shifts with the RM present. The platform has to survive the rigour drop-off of a uni student on their fourth shift this week.

Assistant restaurant manager (2IC)
First salaried rung		 Mid 20s onwards. First salaried rung — significant employee commitment. High Most reliable operational leader inside the store — "in the trenches" alongside crew and shift managers. Materially more rigorous than the average shift manager. Real engagement with quality and process. Steps in to run shifts when RM not present. Incident investigator / corrective-action owner after capture — the persona Donesafe routes work to. Hands-on, professional demeanour, will do any task. The rigour anchor on the floor. Same as licensee.

2IC is the role most likely to actually close investigations and follow through on corrective actions. If the platform makes investigation closure clean, this is the persona who will notice and become an internal advocate.

Likely the 2IC also takes on one of the department manager hats (see row 4b) — role boundaries are blurry.

Role blur

, Assistant Restaurant Manager and senior Shift Manager are blurry. Realistically:

  • The most senior, motivated shift managers likely wear a department manager hat alongside shift mgr duties.
  • The 2IC may also wear one of the department hats.
  • Overlap with assistant manager and senior shift managers is the norm.

So this row represents the role, not a discrete headcount.

Why this matters for the pitch

The three Department Manager tracks map directly onto:

  • QSC pillars (Quality / Service / + the People dimension)
  • McD corporate teams (Quality Assurance / Customer Service / Employee Relations)

One platform showing it can serve all three lenses simultaneously is the "speak in McD's language across departments" angle.
5 ▶
Restaurant manager
"Leader of the restaurant"
Primary demo persona
 Late 20s (high achievers) up to 40s. Salaried. Working hours closer to traditional business hours (~9-to-5 Mon–Fri) — distinct from the shift-based work of their subordinates. Highest among store staff Presence dramatically improves rigour and performance across the floor — the "RM is in" effect is observable. Total store P&L. Hire/fire. People development pipeline. Audit and compliance ownership. Less hands-on than 2IC — more administrative / strategic / vendor / office work; steps in during peak. Owns the upward relationship with Supervisor (licensee) or Ops Consultant (McOpCo). Same as licensee.

The RM's working pattern (traditional business hours) is materially different from the shift-based crew and shift managers below them. This matters for the demo: their natural interface is desktop in an office, not iPad on the floor — but the platform has to serve both modes seamlessly.

Pitch implication: If the RM can't pull clean reports for their morning admin block, the platform is failing them — even if the in-restaurant capture flow is great.

Area supervisor
Between the store and licensee/corporate
Primary demo persona
 30s–40s. Very mobile — covers a cluster of restaurants. Run off their feet — long hours, too much to do in too little time.

Real AU example: Mathew Peters — 15 stores Canberra/NSW, 15 RMs, 120 shift mgrs, 1,400 staff total. Ben knows him loosely.		 High Run-off-their-feet workload, multi-site accountability, direct line to the licensee = high engagement. Style varies by licensee culture — two archetypes ("tight ship" vs "cost minimisation"), see detail panel — but care factor for the role itself is consistently high. Profit, QSC, Sales, People across the region. Manages Supervisors (and through them, RMs). Final operational escalation under the licensee. Strategic capital and people decisions for the group.
Ben directly observed this: his Canberra store changed hands to a family with ~15 stores — owner and 2IC "essentially never on the floor". [Ben]		 McOpCo equivalent: Operations Consultant. Paul (Gong): "operations consultant is what we call the area manager for mccopco". Same scope, corporate paycheck. "Doing everything on their phone… they don't necessarily have the time or flexibility to jump on a desktop". #2 demo persona per Chris's briefing pack.

The two archetypes

  • "Tight ship": enforces stringent standards; functions as brand ambassador; high care factor; pushes adherence.
  • "Cost minimisation": prioritises cost reduction to maximise owner profit, sometimes at the expense of operational excellence; lower care factor on standards.

McOpCo Ops Consultants almost never present the second archetype (no profit-motive friction — they're corporate salaried). Franchise Area Supervisors can present either.

Why this is SC's highest-leverage persona

  • Visit multiple stores → multi-site audit data is their currency.
  • Mobile-first by necessity → lightweight tooling wins.
  • Time-pressed → reward any tool that genuinely saves them effort.
  • Natural carrier of multi-site data back to licensee / corporate.
  • The persona where "less burdensome compliance" lands hardest.

Licensee
Owner-operator / "franchisee"		 Highly variable. 200+ licensees in AU running ~80% of ~1,200 stores. Spectrum: single-store owner-operators just out of the 12-month unpaid qualification → multi-generational families running 15–20 stores with full Area Supervisor + Supervisor + RM structure beneath them. Extreme $1–2M of their own capital sunk in the franchise; 12 months of unpaid full-time training to qualify; P&L is entirely theirs. Profit-motive friction shapes their lens — they bear the cost of compliance and capture the upside from non-compliance (see Framework Triangulation). P&L for their store(s). Capital reinvestment. Employer of record for all their crew (not McD AU). Brand standard compliance. Per licensee agreement, must work in-restaurant regularly. For large licensees: oversight of the multi-layer org beneath them. Licensee-only role. No McOpCo equivalent. Closest analogue is McD AU corporate leadership (Summer Foley / Chris Caulfield in Paul's org). Kathy wants licensees logging in to see "widgets, pies, graphs" for their stores — the bridge between "corporate buys it" and "licensees actually use it". [Gong]

Profit-motive friction

The structural reason franchise rollouts of corporate-mandated platforms are harder than McOpCo rollouts. The entity bearing compliance cost is the same entity capturing the upside from non-compliance — predictable friction, not a character defect.

Initiatives that work flawlessly in McOpCo cannot be ported into franchise by re-running the same playbook. Franchise sell shifts from "visibility/control" to "tangible workload benefit for frontline management".

Don't sell to them directly

This is a corporate-led RFP. Licensees are recipients of whatever corporate picks. The Kathy dashboard angle ("widgets, pies, graphs") is the bridge — show that the platform makes licensees' lives easier, so they actually use it rather than just tolerating it.
