SCENARIO 01

Fry station overhead cover failure

★ Real anecdote — Ben, Dickson ACT
Shift Manager QSC · Quality Quality Assurance / Safety
Fry station — reference photo Click to see the vats & overhead hood
Situation Mid-shift, non-peak. Crew member working fry station follows the widely-practised informal drainage technique — basket on the retaining hook, bang the handle against the stainless steel hood. This shift, the heavy metal hood cover spanning the six-bay drive-thru fry line detaches from its mounting and swings down, hanging precariously over a vat of hot oil. Crew is immobilised. Near-miss: full hood collapse into the vat would mean a massive oil splash and food contamination across the line.
Current state — Donesafe / verbal Crew yells over to the shift manager, both hands full mid-fry. Shift manager might jot a note. More likely: forgotten amidst the demands of the shift. Mounting gets re-secured later by maintenance with no record of cause. The informal banging procedure that caused the mount fatigue persists store-wide — and propagates crew-to-crew. Same failure recurs in 6 months, untraceable.
Suggested SC angle Shift manager opens Issue on the in-hand iPad in <15s — voice-records description, photo of the dangling cover, AI prefills incident type and severity. Two auto-actions: (1) maintenance ticket for the mount railing; (2) corrective procedure flag escalating the informal "banging" technique for cross-store comms. The systemic cause gets surfaced, not just the symptom.
Why this resonates: equipment hazard PLUS unsanctioned crew technique on the same record. The "verbal-to-forgotten" pattern is Paul's exact stated pain.
SCENARIO 02

Summernats — fry unit oil leak during peak

★ Real anecdote — Ben, Dickson ACT
Shift Manager QSC · Cleanliness QSC · Quality Quality Assurance / Safety
Situation Annual Summernats weekend — one of the busiest local-event peaks of the year in Canberra. Crew assigned single-station for the duration (I was 100% on fry). Oil starts leaking significantly from the fry unit; a slip hazard spreads across the floor. The drive-thru is stacked back to the street, headset chatter constant, PCC streak at risk. Workflow physically cannot pause.
Current state — Donesafe / verbal Standard Donesafe flow asks the shift manager to step away from the line, find a device, navigate a form with free-text fields. That doesn't happen during Summernats. Manager throws a wet floor sign down, yells "watch the floor" and keeps moving. Hazard goes unlogged. If someone slips later, there's no record this was a known issue.
Suggested SC angle Manager pulls phone, taps Issue, says "oil leak fry unit three spreading across the floor" — voice-to-text + AI prefill + photo, submitted without leaving the line. Auto-routes to equipment maintenance + safety team. Trend data later shows this fryer unit is a repeat offender on peak days.
Why this resonates: a concrete, named local peak event Paul/Kathy will recognise instantly — and it concretises Paul's "minimise administrative friction in restaurant" priority better than any abstract example.
SCENARIO 03

Area supervisor — multi-site walk-through, four stores in a day

Constructed · Paul's stated mobile persona
Area Supervisor QSC · Service QSC · Cleanliness QSC · Quality Ops Quality Assurance
Situation Area supervisor visiting four stores across her cluster in a single day. Store 2: Happy Meal merch display damaged in the front-of-house. Store 3: a crew member working with no hat (uniform breach). Store 4: dining area unsanitary at 2pm. She has 25 minutes between stops, drives between them, and is — as the KB puts it — run off her feet.
Current state — iPhone Notes + memory Notes typed into iPhone Notes between stops. Sometimes a text to the restaurant manager from the car park. Back at the office Friday she tries to remember everything. Half the non-conformities never make it to Donesafe. No trend visible across her cluster — and the merch display is still damaged the following week.
Suggested SC angle Mobile-first inspection on her own phone during the walk-through. Tap-select non-conformity from a pre-built list, photo, auto-assign to that store's RM with deadline. Two minutes per finding. Cluster-level dashboard shows trends by store, by non-conformity type. She leaves the floor with everything captured — no end-of-week catch-up.
Why this resonates: Paul called out ops consultants and area supervisors as the mobile use case in the Gong — "doing everything on their phone, a lot of time around on the road." Strongest possible visual contrast to the "pin a browser tab" status quo Kathy described.
SCENARIO 04

Council health inspector — unannounced visit

Constructed · maps to Kathy's named example
Restaurant Manager QSC · Quality QSC · Cleanliness Quality Assurance
Situation Local council health inspector walks in unannounced, Wednesday 11am, pre-lunch ramp. Restaurant manager hosts the walk-through. Inspector flags two minor non-conformities: one chemical bottle missing its label in the back dock; one cool-room temperature log with a four-hour gap. Verbal "fix it and document it." The formal PDF report lands in the RM's inbox four days later.
Current state — Donesafe RM memorises the two issues on a Post-it. Logs them as a free-text Donesafe incident that night. The inspector's PDF lives in email. No structured record links the visit, the non-conformities, the corrective actions, and the closure evidence. Area supervisor sees the Donesafe entry but never the PDF — and has no view of inspector visits across her cluster.
Suggested SC angle "Health Inspector Visit" inspection template — structured fields, two non-conformities captured live with photos, PDF attached. Auto-generates two corrective actions with owners and deadlines, closes when evidence is uploaded. Area supervisor's dashboard rolls up inspector visits across the cluster. Quality Assurance team has the full chain on one record.
Why this resonates: Kathy named this verbatim in the Gong — "health inspector visits they enter in there as well." Already part of their flow; SC just does it properly end-to-end.
SCENARIO 05

Misconduct allegation — crew vs shift manager

Constructed · maps to Kathy's named example
Restaurant Manager People (no direct QSC equivalent) Employee Relations / Workplace Investigations
Situation A 17-year-old crew member tells a crew trainer they're being routinely assigned the worst flex tasks — toilet duty, cardboard cutting — by one specific shift manager, over multiple shifts. Crew trainer escalates to the 2IC; 2IC escalates to the Restaurant Manager. Allegation is unproven but plausible — and the crew member is under 18.
Current state — Donesafe RM opens a Donesafe incident with a free-text narrative. Visibility can't easily be restricted to the right people. Sharing the record with the Employee Relations corporate team means screenshots or exports. Roster evidence (who was on which flex on which shift) lives separately. No clean audit trail of who accessed what when — critical for a minor.
Suggested SC angle Sensitive-issue template with restricted visibility (RM, 2IC, ER corporate only). Structured fields for allegation, witnesses, evidence. Linked roster snapshots. Full audit trail of access and edits. ER corporate runs the investigation directly off the same record without re-keying.
Why this resonates: Kathy explicitly named "bullying, harassment, misconduct" investigations. Chris flagged in the Gong that McD is one of Australia's biggest employers of young people — confidentiality controls are the differentiator.
SCENARIO 06

Foreign object complaint — Big Mac, drive-thru returner

Constructed · maps to Kathy's named example
Shift Manager → Restaurant Manager QSC · Quality Customer Service Quality Assurance
Situation Customer drives back through 20 minutes after picking up their order, claims they found a small metal fragment in their Big Mac. Shift manager remakes the order and offers a refund. Customer leaves saying "I'll be in touch." Could be a clamshell grill artefact; could be slicer wear; could be unfounded. Either way it's both a customer service issue and a food safety incident.
Current state — Donesafe + corporate inbox Shift manager fills a customer complaint form. Quality Assurance has no line of sight until someone exports data weekly. Customer Service team gets a parallel inbound through the corporate complaints inbox — different thread, same incident. Two records, no equipment linkage, no traceability back to grill ID or slicer maintenance history.
Suggested SC angle Single incident record. Photo of object if available, structured "object type / colour / size" fields if not. Auto-routes to BOTH Customer Service and Quality Assurance with the same context. Linked to equipment IDs so QA can pull maintenance history with one click. Trend reporting surfaces foreign-object incidents by equipment across the network.
Why this resonates: Kathy's exact example — "a food safety incident, they put it through Donesafe… certain questions which then they would investigate on their end." Two corporate teams touching one record is the demonstration angle she described.
SCENARIO 07

Walk-in freezer door — intermittent latch failure

Constructed · combines KB equipment + verbal-to-forgotten pattern
Shift Manager (close) QSC · Quality Quality Assurance Ops
Situation Closing shift manager notices the walk-in freezer door doesn't fully latch on the first push — needs a second nudge. Twice tonight, crew found it ajar after stock runs. If it stays ajar overnight: full freezer thaw, thousands of dollars of stock loss, a HACCP issue, and a board-level write-up. Equipment maintenance is third-party, overnight-only.
Current state — Post-it & verbal Shift manager writes "FREEZER DOOR DODGY" on a Post-it stuck to the door. Tells the RM verbally the next morning. RM tests the door in the moment, finds it latches fine, mentally dismisses as "crew didn't push hard enough." Latch fully fails nine days later mid-Saturday rush.
Suggested SC angle Quick issue capture: photo + voice — "freezer door not latching first push, recurring." Auto-routes to the third-party maintenance contractor with a priority flag. Auto-escalation if not actioned in 24 hours. Trend data exposes recurring latch failures by equipment model across the cluster.
Why this resonates: the verbal-to-forgotten pattern in its purest form, with food-safety stakes — directly attacks the structural failure mode Paul described and the trend-visibility gap Kathy described, on one scenario.
