1. Corporate ↔ Licensee dynamic

McOpCo = corporate-operated restaurants (incl. Thornleigh flagship). Licensee = McD's term for franchisee — the vast majority. AU split unconfirmed; global ~95% franchised is a useful anchor. Licensees range from single-store operators to families holding ~15 stores.

What corporate can / can't push

Corporate CAN unilaterally push

  • New tech platforms across the estate (Donesafe rolled this way ~10 yrs ago)
  • New incident / hazard workflows & forms
  • Mandatory training modules in MeTime/MeOut; SOC competency instruments
  • Standardised audits, QSC checklists, food-safety protocols
  • System-level compliance (e.g. KDS lockout on youth-labour breach)

Corporate CAN'T (easily) force

  • How rigorously a licensee actually uses the system day-to-day
  • Severity classification on incidents — Licensee stores get guidance & definitions, not enforcement (vs McOpCo auto-flagged critical) [Paul, Gong]
  • Local hiring & scheduling decisions
  • Discretionary spend on top of corporate stack

Profit-motive friction — the structural challenge

Licensees bear the cost of compliance and capture the upside from non-compliance — so drift is structural, not a character defect. What works flawlessly in McOpCo (salaried staff executing) won't port to franchise via the same playbook.

McOpCo is not a profit centre

McOpCo stores are controlled operational laboratories — proving grounds for new systems, standards, and equipment. Staff are salaried corporate employees, so no profit-motive resistance to new directives. Implication: McOpCo gives the cleanest evaluation signal and the easiest rollout — but commercial justification depends on franchise adoption.

2. How safety flows today

In-store hierarchy: crew → crew trainer → shift manager → assistant restaurant manager / 2IC + department managers (Customer Experience / People Performance / Product Quality) → restaurant manager → area supervisor (mobile, multi-site) → ops consultants / corporate.

The current-state pain that wins the room

Dominant failure mode: verbal-to-shift-manager-to-forgotten.

  1. Incident happens (oil spill, near-miss, equipment fault)
  2. Crew verbally tells the shift manager mid-task, hands full
  3. Shift manager might jot a note — more often, forgotten under shift pressure
  4. No formal log. No corrective action. Systemic cause never surfaces.

The system has to make it faster to log than to forget. Voice capture, instant photo, AI-populated fields, low/zero mandatory fields at capture — these directly attack this failure mode.

Why SOCs (Station Observation Checklists) matter to the McD ear

The "competency sheet" is the SOC — McD's named, standardised competency-assessment instrument. Crew trainers use SOCs to certify crew on each station. Paper-and-binder historically, migrating to iPad. Speaking in "SOC" language signals fluency. Replacing the paper SOC workflow is a concrete, repeatable wedge that scales across every station in every store.

4. For Monday's demo

What this means for the demo

  • Lead with Shift Manager & Restaurant Manager on phone/iPad, in-restaurant. Not desktop. Not safety team.
  • Show different lenses on the same record — restaurant capture → ops consultant / supervisor review → safety team analytics → CS / Food Safety / ER branching. Mirrors how they actually work.

Who's in the room

The CFT is McD AU corporate — Paul (Insurance & Safety), the safety team, plus daily-user departments (CS, Food Safety/QA, SIM, Ops, ER, IT). They interact with store-level personas via systems and reporting — they don't live on the floor. Show them the in-restaurant experience their counterparts would have, then how that data surfaces into the corporate lens they live in.

Selling structure

Corporate-led RFP. Paul owns it, the CFT advises, corporate decides. Licensees were consulted (~300 feedback items) but don't hold a vote. Win corporate, then make the in-restaurant experience good enough that licensees don't quietly abandon it.
